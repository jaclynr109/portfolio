Subtheme: Exploring Corporate Owners and Evictions

Data Overview

The dataset used in this analysis is the corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates in Boston neighborhoods for 2004-2024, with census demographic data layered in. The data was sourced from City of Boston's Property Assessment Data, the city's dataset for neighborhood boundaries (approximated by 2020 census tracts), and the US Census Bureau. While I was initially interested in exploring how demographic trends changed over time- along with corporate ownership rates and housing unit vacancies, to understand gentrification- the census data was discovered to be the same (static) for each year in the dataset. My inquiries were therefore limited to exploring vacancies and corporate ownership over time, while demographic data was used to contextualize the findings.

Overall Analysis Questions

The report on Housing Justice Beyond the Emergency Analysis by Homes for All Massachusetts and researchers at MIT concludes that accross the Commonwealth, concentrated ownership by corporate landlords was strongly associated with higher eviction filing rates, while presence of live in landlords as associated with housing stability. Places with larger percentages of Black and Latinx renters, in particular, tended to see higher rates of eviction filing. The report notes a large body of research that demonstrates corporate property ownership exacerbates housing injustice. I am broadly interested in exploring if these same trends emerge in the dataset provided when looking more granularly at Boston neighborhoods. From this motivation, I examine data with the following questions in mind:

  1. How have rates of owner occupancy and corporate ownership changed over time in Boston?
  2. Are corporate ownership rates higher in neighborhoods with more diverse populations?
  3. How does rising corporate ownership affect housing vacancies?
  4. What features of neighborhoods might affect the relationship between corporate ownership and vacancy?

Data Validation and Consistency Checks

    Prior to visual analysis, I conducted a number of internal consistency checks to verify structure and completeness of the dataset. Through this, I ensured that:
  1. Occupied and vacant units sum to total units
  2. Age-based population counts sum to total population
  3. Racial category counts sum to neighborhood-level totals
  4. Group quarters subcategories (residents in college, military, corrections, juvenile, nursing, other institution, other non-institution housing) sum to total group quarters population
    5. No systematic accounting discrepancies were detected. The dataset contains 441 neighborhood-year observations spanning 21 neighborhoods over 2004–2024.

    Discoveries & Insights

Summary

This project examined how corporate housing ownership varies across Boston neighborhoods and to what extent it is associated with racial diversity, housing vacancy, and housing unit composition (via collegial and other institutional populations). Corporate ownership throughout Boston has increased over time on average and is unevenly distributed, with high concentrations in neighborhoods such as Fenway and South Boston Waterfront. The relationship between corporate ownership and vacancy is not consistent across neighborhoods. Some high corporate ownership neighborhoods exhibit elevated vacancy, but others do not. The comparison between Fenway and South Boston Waterfront highlights this divergence and suggests that ownership concentration alone does not determine vacancy outcomes. Exploring potential mechanisms, this analysis indicates that the presence of college student renters may help explain these differences. Fenway’s notable student population appears to be associated with lower vacancy despite high corporate ownership, whereas South Boston Waterfront’s relatively limited student presence coincides with higher vacancy. In comparison, non-college institutional populations do not show a strong relationship with vacancy. Overall, these findings suggest that the effects of corporate ownership are context-dependent and likely affected by local housing demand rather than being uniformly associated with vacancy or neighborhood racial diversity. This analysis is descriptive and does not establish causality, but does highlight the importance of neighborhood-level dynamics in understanding the implications of corporate housing ownership.
